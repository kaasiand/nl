Nouns: the basics

Traditionally, Dutch has three grammatical genders: masculine, feminine and neuter. However, like in Swedish and Danish, the distinction between the masculine and feminine genders has largely disappeared in Dutch (except in the south). In practice, it's fine if you treat Dutch as having only two grammatical genders: common (m⁠/⁠f) and neuter.

Those actual names aren't too important. In Dutch we often just call them de‑woorden and het‑woorden, after the definite article they use. The indefinite article is always (unstressed) een, and is not used with plurals.

Most plurals are formed with either ‑en or ‑s. Without exception, plurals always work like de words.

A small number of words instead use ‑eren for plurals (kinderen, like English children), while many Latin-based words may use Latin endings (museum → musea, politicus → politici, crisis → crises⁠ ⁠).

Diminutives

Most nouns in Dutch can be turned into diminutives, typically ending in ‑(t )⁠⁠ ⁠je. In the south and in female given names, ‑ke often occurs too. With the exception of given names, diminutives are always neuter and thus take the definite article het in the singular. The plurals of diminutives are always formed with ‑s.

Some words may undergo a vowel change or lengthening in plural and/or diminutive forms (schip → schepen/⁠scheepje; factor → factoren⁠ ⁠).

Compounds

Compound nouns are generally written as single words in Dutch, though you'll often see people write them separately (due to English influence; possibly also web searchability). The grammatical gender of a compound is the same as that of its last part.

Unusual compounds may optionally be written with a hyphen in between, for legibility. A hyphen is required for compounds with initialisms (wc-papier), and compounds which juxtapose vowels that might be misread as a different vowel or diphthong (vanille-extract, but vanillearoma).
