Nouns

Traditionally, Dutch has three grammatical genders: masculine, feminine and neuter. However, like in Swedish and Danish, the distinction between the masculine and feminine genders has largely disappeared in Dutch, except in the south. In practice, it's fine if you treat Dutch as having only two grammatical genders: common (m⁠/⁠f) and neuter.

Those actual names aren't too important. In Dutch we often just call them de‑woorden and het‑woorden, after the definite article they use. Plurals always use de, without exception.

The indefinite article is always een, and is not used for the plural.
